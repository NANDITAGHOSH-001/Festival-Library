DIWALI

What is Diwali?

Diwali, also known as Deepavali or the Festival of Lights, is India’s most significant autumn celebration, spanning five days—typically in October or November. The name comes from Sanskrit Deepavali, meaning a “row of lamps.” During Diwali, people light diyas (clay oil lamps), decorate homes with rangoli (colorful floor art), wear new clothes, exchange sweets and gifts, and set off fireworks to symbolize the victory of light over darkness and good over evil.

Historical Roots & Significance:

Diwali, known as the Festival of Lights, is a joyous five-day celebration in October–November that symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. It marks Lord Rama’s triumphant return to Ayodhya after defeating Ravana, as homes and streets glow with diyas and colorful rangoli art. Festivities include prayers to Goddess Lakshmi for prosperity, family gatherings, feasts, gift-giving, traditional oil baths, and fireworks . Though rooted in Hindu mythology, Diwali is also observed by Jains, Sikhs, and some Buddhists, making it a culturally inclusive festival celebrated worldwide

Rituals and Symbolisms:

  • Midnight Worship on Amavasya – Rituals begin at the darkest hour—the new moon night—to invoke Kali’s most powerful, transformative energy.
  • Altar Setup & Offerings – Idols or images of Kali are adorned with red hibiscus, sweets, rice, and lentils, symbolizing both devotion and the destruction of negativity.
  • Mantra Chanting & Aarti –Devotees chant sacred Kali mantras—often 108 times—while performing aarti to invite her blessings and dispel ignorance.
  • Symbolic Sacrifices & Tantric Practices – Offerings may include meat, fish, alcohol, or symbolic substitutes (like pumpkins) in tantric traditions, representing surrender and taboos overcome.
