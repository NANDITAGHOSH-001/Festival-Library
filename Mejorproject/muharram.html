MUHARRAM

Al-Muharram is the first and one of the four sacred months in the Islamic calendar during which warfare is forbidden. The 10th day of this month, known as Ashura, holds deep religious significance. For Sunni Muslims, Ashura commemorates the day Prophet Moses parted the Red Sea and saved the Israelites, observed through optional fasting and expressions of gratitude. For Shia Muslims, it is a solemn day of mourning, marking the martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali, the grandson of Prophet Muhammad, who was killed in the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE. Shia communities observe intense mourning rituals during the first ten days of Muharram, especially on Ashura. Additionally, during this month, the Al-Aqsa Mosque in Jerusalem was initially designated as the first direction of Muslim prayer.

Origins

Muharram, meaning "sacred," is the first month of the Islamic calendar and can have up to thirty days. It is one of the four sacred months in which warfare is strictly forbidden—a tradition that dates back to pre-Islamic times. The name "Muharram" is derived from the older term "Muharram Safar," used in the ancient Arab calendar to distinguish between two months: Safar I (considered sacred) and Safar II (not sacred). Over time, the adjective "Muharram" evolved to become the proper name of the first month of the Islamic year.

Ashura

Ashura, observed on the 10th of Muharram, holds deep significance in Islam. For Sunni Muslims, it commemorates events like the parting of the Red Sea by Moses, Noah’s Ark landing, and other blessings, celebrated through fasting and pious acts. In some Sunni regions, it also includes festive traditions. For Shia Muslims, Ashura is a solemn day of mourning for the martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali, the Prophet Muhammad’s grandson, at the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE. Shia communities mark the day with processions, gatherings, and reenactments, viewing it as a protest against injustice and a form of spiritual struggle.
