DURGA PUJA

What is Durga Puja?

Durga Puja is one of the most revered Hindu festivals, especially celebrated in West Bengal, Assam, Odisha, Bihar, and other regions across India and the world. The festival honors Goddess Durga—the divine mother of the universe—victorious over the buffalo demon Mahishasura. This victory represents the eternal triumph of good over evil, light over darkness, and justice over injustice.

Historical Roots & Significance:

Durga Puja evolved from ancient Vedic roots into today’s grand community celebration. Its earliest mentions date back to Vedic hymns and the Devi Mahatmya (circa 400–600 CE), nurturing devotion to the divine feminine. What began as private rituals in royal and zamindar households—highlighted by Raja Nabakrishna Deb’s public Puja in 1757—gradually transformed through community-driven barowari and sarbojanin traditions from the late 18th century onward. Nowadays, Durga Puja transcends spirituality to become a cultural festival—celebrating unparalleled artistry in elaborate pandals, vibrant music and dance performances, engaging theatre, and regional delicacies—unifying communities in worship, celebration, and lifelong memories

Rituals and Symbolisms:

  • Bodhon – The ritual of Bodhon marks the invocation of Goddess Durga on the evening of Shashti (the sixth day). It symbolizes the awakening of the divine feminine energy and the beginning of the main Puja days.
  • Aarti & Mantra Chanting – Morning & evening light ceremonies with mantras to purify and bless.
  • Pushpanjali – Devotees offer flowers while chanting mantras, seeking blessings.
  • Sindoor Khela –Married women smear vermillion on the goddess and each other on Dashami, symbolizing auspiciousness and sisterhood.
  • Bhog & Prasad – Shared offerings like khichuri, labra, and sweets, blessed and distributed among devotees.
